The World Cup Is Here, and America Is Finally Being Tested as a Soccer Country

The 2026 World Cup is testing whether America's enthusiasm for soccer is structural or situational — and the honest answer is more complicated than either camp wants to admit.

June 27, 2026 · 4 min read
Share

In 1994, the United States hosted the FIFA World Cup for the first time. European and South American press were openly skeptical — this was a country where soccer could barely sustain a professional league, where fans sometimes had to be handed flyers outside stadiums explaining what offside was. The tournament was technically a success. Stadiums filled. Then the World Cup left, and American soccer went mostly back to being a niche.

Thirty-two years later, it's back. Sixteen cities across the US, Canada, and Mexico are hosting 104 matches. An estimated 10 million visitors are arriving in American cities this summer alone. And the question that's been hanging over all of it — the one American soccer fans have been asking and nervously deflecting since the bid was won — is whether this country has actually become a soccer nation, or whether it's just hosting a very large party.

The 1994 gap

The thing about 1994 is that it worked despite American soccer, not because of it. The US team was there because they were the hosts. MLS didn't even exist yet — it launched in 1996 as a direct legacy project from the tournament. The country's best soccer players were playing abroad, mostly quietly, and not many people could name them.

What's changed in the thirty years since is hard to summarize neatly, but a few things stand out. MLS now has 30 teams and is in its fourth decade. Youth soccer participation in the United States is among the highest in the world, which means a generation of adults grew up playing the game and a generation of kids grew up watching parents who cared about it. The US women's team has won four World Cups and built something close to a real fanbase in the process. The men's team, after the humiliating failure to qualify for the 2018 World Cup, reached the knockout rounds in 2022 and drew against England in a group stage match that was actually tense.

None of that is the soccer culture you'd find in Brazil or Germany. But it's not 1994 either.

What's actually happening in the cities

The 11 US host cities this summer are doing something they've never done before: hosting FIFA Fan Festivals — public watch events where fans without tickets can gather to watch matches on massive screens alongside food, music, and a genuinely chaotic mix of international supporters who would otherwise have nothing in common.

At Rockefeller Center in New York, FIFA built a temporary pitch and a dedicated Fan Village. Kansas City — a football-and-barbecue city of half a million — is expecting 650,000 visitors over the course of the tournament. Dallas, Los Angeles, Miami, and Seattle are all hosting matches, each of them now temporarily a city with an international soccer identity grafted onto it.

The cultural mixing this produces is one of the consistently underreported stories of every World Cup. When 32 countries' fanbases end up in the same streets, the same transit systems, and the same bars, you get a kind of compressed cultural encounter that doesn't really happen anywhere else. The US gets some version of that in normal times — it's a big, diverse country — but not like this, not this concentrated and this loud.

Whether the feeling lasts

The honest version of the American soccer question isn't whether people are enjoying this World Cup. They clearly are. It's what happens in September, when the tournament is over and the local MLS team in whatever city you live in is probably not very good, and the Premier League is back across an eight-hour time difference.

The 1994 pattern was: massive event, genuine enthusiasm, then gradual drift back to normal. MLS launching two years later helped prevent a total dropoff, but it still took another two decades for the league to build real traction.

The 2026 version has some structural advantages that 1994 didn't. Streaming has made watching European leagues trivially easy, which means there's now a pipeline of fans who followed the game internationally before the World Cup started. The generation attending these matches grew up watching Premier League soccer on weekend mornings. They already have their favorite clubs.

Whether that translates into sustained attention to American soccer after the tournament is the real test. The World Cup is a great party. The question is what you do the morning after.
