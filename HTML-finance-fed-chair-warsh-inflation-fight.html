New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh Signals No Retreat From Inflation Fight, Rattling Rate-Cut Hopes

In his first major address on the global stage, Warsh told central bankers the Fed "will deliver price stability" — a stance that pushed up the odds of a rate hike and unsettled markets on the first trading day of the third quarter.

July 1, 2026 · 4 min read
Share

Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh said Wednesday that the central bank remains committed to bringing inflation back down to its 2% target, all but ruling out the interest rate cuts President Trump has publicly demanded. Speaking at a central banking conference in Sintra, Portugal, Warsh said that anyone expecting the Fed to tolerate higher inflation "would be disappointed. We're going to deliver price stability." Wall Street read the comments as hawkish — meaning Warsh favored keeping rates higher to fight inflation, rather than cutting them to boost growth — and futures markets moved to price in higher odds of a rate increase later this year.

Background

Warsh took over as Fed chair on May 22, replacing Jerome Powell after Trump nominated him in January. During his confirmation campaign, Warsh had called for lower interest rates — comments that fueled expectations he would tilt the Fed toward the White House's preference for cheaper borrowing. Since taking the job, however, he has moved the opposite way, emphasizing the Fed's independence and its unfinished task of taming prices.

That task has gotten harder, not easier. Inflation climbed to 4.2% in May, a three-year high, driven largely by a spike in gas prices tied to the war between Israel and Iran. A ceasefire has since brought gas prices down, and Warsh said Wednesday that measures of where the public and financial markets expect inflation to head have started to ease. The Fed's benchmark rate — the interest rate that influences borrowing costs across the economy — has sat at 3.5% to 3.75% since its June 16–17 meeting, where policymakers left rates unchanged but revealed a split outlook: nearly half of the Fed's 19 officials said they wanted higher rates before year's end.

What Happened

Warsh declined Wednesday to say whether the Fed's next meeting, scheduled for July 28–29, will produce a rate hike. He cited his general opposition to "forward guidance," the practice of central bankers signaling future moves in advance. "The tactics, the strategy, and the rest, that's still to come," he said.

He also addressed artificial intelligence, arguing that over time AI would expand the economy's productive capacity and ease inflationary pressure, while acknowledging that in the short run, heavy spending on AI infrastructure and chips is pushing prices up, not down. Investors had already been pricing in a possible rate hike as soon as September, moving the expected rate from roughly 3.6% to about 3.9%. After Warsh's remarks, the odds of a move as early as July's meeting rose sharply, according to the CME Group's FedWatch tool, a widely watched gauge that converts trading activity into implied probabilities for the Fed's next move.

"We've been an independent central bank for a very long time... you're going to see no changes to that."

Reactions

Markets responded within hours. Futures tied to two major stock indexes, the Nasdaq 100 and the S&P 500, slipped roughly 0.5% to 0.8% in early trading as investors recalibrated expectations for borrowing costs against a stock rally built partly on AI enthusiasm. The "Magnificent Seven" — the seven large technology companies whose stocks have driven much of the market's recent gains — had already shed an estimated $2.3 trillion in combined value in June amid growing doubts about whether AI spending is translating into profits. Trump, who has pushed publicly for lower rates to ease borrowing costs, did not immediately respond to Warsh's comments, though he has previously praised recent inflation data as "great" while continuing to press the Fed to cut.

What This Means

For most Americans, the practical stakes are straightforward: if the Fed raises rates instead of cutting them, borrowing stays expensive. Mortgages, auto loans, and credit card rates are all tied, directly or indirectly, to the Fed's benchmark rate, so a hike would make each more costly just as many households are already contending with elevated prices. It would also test the durability of the stock market's recent gains, particularly in the AI-heavy tech sector, which has benefited from expectations of easier money. Warsh's message was that the Fed intends to finish the job on inflation even if that means short-term pain — a bet that the economy, still adding jobs at a solid pace, can absorb it. Whether that bet pays off may hinge on data due later this week, including Thursday's jobs report, which economists expect to show unemployment holding at a still-low 4.3%.

The bottom line: Warsh's message was that the Fed intends to finish the job on inflation even if that means short-term pain for borrowers and markets alike — a bet that a still-solid labor market can absorb.
