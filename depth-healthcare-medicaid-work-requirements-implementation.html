What 80 Hours a Month Actually Means

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's Medicaid work requirements aren't a future policy fight — they're machinery that starts turning in July, and the research on similar experiments suggests the people who lose coverage won't be the people who stopped working.

June 27, 2026 · 12 min read · 3,000 words
Share

In Nebraska, Medicaid work requirements went into effect May 1. In Montana, they start July 1. In Iowa, December 1. In Arkansas, there's a soft launch on July 1 where the paperwork machinery will begin turning, though the state has pledged not to disenroll anyone until January. Dozens more states are scheduled to begin enforcement January 1, 2027.

These are not distant policy projections. They are dates on a calendar. The administrative machinery that will determine whether millions of low-income Americans keep their health insurance is being assembled right now, state by state, in agencies that have never implemented anything like this before. The federal law that requires it — the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025 — was projected by the Congressional Budget Office to cut $911 billion from Medicaid over ten years. Work requirements account for $326 billion of that.[1][2]

The people who will lose coverage in the coming years are not abstractions. They are the 72 million Americans currently enrolled in Medicaid. The question this piece examines is not whether coverage losses will occur — the CBO said they will — but how, exactly, that happens: the administrative mechanisms through which the law's text becomes a disenrollment notice in someone's mailbox.

The Law's Architecture

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act conditions Medicaid eligibility for adults in the ACA Medicaid expansion group on meeting "community engagement requirements" — 80 hours per month of work, job training, education, volunteer activity, or similar qualifying activities. The federal deadline for most states to begin implementation is January 1, 2027, though states can opt to start earlier, and several have.[3]

Exemptions exist on paper. The law excludes individuals who are medically frail, pregnant, caregivers of dependent children, full-time students, and people with certain documented disabilities. On June 1, 2026, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services issued an interim final rule providing guidance on how states should determine "medical frailty" — the broadest and most consequential exemption category.[4]

The interim final rule matters because the definition of medical frailty is inherently subjective and the law doesn't specify it with precision. Under the CMS guidance, states are directed to use Medicaid claims data — ICD-10 diagnostic codes and CPT service codes — to identify individuals who may be medically frail and therefore exempt. Ten states have reported identifying both specific diagnostic codes and service codes to confirm medical frailty; many more have not yet reached that stage of implementation planning.[4]

The variation between states is significant and is not accidental. The law gives states substantial discretion in how they implement both the verification requirements and the exemptions. A state that defines medical frailty broadly — using a wide range of diagnostic codes, requiring minimal documentation, and proactively notifying eligible individuals of their exempt status — will produce very different coverage outcomes than a state that defines it narrowly, requires extensive documentation renewal, and places the burden of proving exemption entirely on the enrollee.[5]

What Arkansas Taught Us

The closest precedent for what is about to unfold nationally is Arkansas's 2018 experiment with Medicaid work requirements — the first time any state had conditioned Medicaid coverage on employment. Arkansas's program required Medicaid recipients in the expansion group to report at least 80 hours per month of qualifying activity. In the months it operated before a federal court struck it down, approximately 18,000 people lost coverage.

The research that followed, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, produced a result that surprised many of the program's advocates: the people who lost coverage were not, in any meaningful sense, failing to work. They were, in large numbers, actually employed — but they hadn't complied with the reporting requirements. They had trouble accessing the online reporting portal. They didn't receive the notices. They didn't understand what was required of them. A substantial share met the work requirement substantively but failed to meet it procedurally.[6]

This distinction is critical to understanding the coverage loss projections. The CBO's estimate that work requirements will reduce Medicaid enrollment by 5.2 million adults by 2034 is not primarily a projection of how many people will stop working. Most Medicaid recipients who are capable of working already do. A 2019 KFF analysis found that among non-elderly adult Medicaid recipients who weren't exempt for a specific reason, a significant majority were already working, in school, or caregiving — activities that would qualify under the law.

The coverage losses come from people who qualify but can't navigate the documentation requirements: who work informal jobs without pay stubs, who have caregiving responsibilities that are real but not officially documented, who have medical conditions that should qualify as medical frailty but don't get captured by the ICD-10 codes their state is using for auto-exemption, who miss a renewal deadline because they moved and the notice went to an old address.

The Reporting Machinery and Its Failure Modes

The law requires states to verify at both application and renewal that enrollees meet work requirements or qualify for an exemption. This creates a periodic reporting cycle — typically monthly or quarterly, depending on state implementation — in which enrollees must affirmatively document their hours or their exempt status. Missing a reporting deadline, even if the underlying activity qualifies, can trigger disenrollment.[3]

The administrative friction this introduces is not hypothetical. Medicaid enrollment and renewal processes have a well-documented history of coverage losses that are administrative rather than substantive — people losing coverage not because they became ineligible but because they failed to return a form, didn't receive a renewal notice, or couldn't get through to the agency's phone line. This phenomenon, sometimes called "churn," is persistent even in states with relatively functional enrollment systems.[7]

Work requirement reporting adds a new, more frequent documentation obligation on top of existing renewal requirements. For someone working a part-time retail job while managing a chronic condition and caring for a child — a description that fits a substantial portion of the Medicaid expansion population — logging monthly work hours, obtaining verification from employers, and submitting that documentation through an online portal is not a trivial administrative task. It's one more thing that can go wrong.

States have choices about how to reduce this friction. They can use existing wage data from state unemployment insurance records to automatically verify employment, reducing the burden on enrollees. Several states are planning to do this; several are not. States can send proactive notices about the requirement and offer in-person assistance; states differ here too. The patchwork nature of implementation means that the same enrollee — working the same hours, living with the same medical condition — may keep or lose coverage depending on which side of a state line they live on.[5]

The Fiscal Logic and Its Hidden Costs

The $326 billion in projected ten-year savings from work requirements rests on a specific assumption: that a substantial number of people who would otherwise be covered will either not meet the requirement or fail to document that they do, and will therefore be disenrolled. This is not a side effect of the policy. It is the mechanism through which the savings are produced.

This creates a fiscal logic worth stating plainly. The savings materialize when people lose coverage. If the administrative implementation is so smooth and accessible that every eligible person maintains coverage and every exempt person is correctly identified, the savings largely disappear. The program's fiscal success, as measured by CBO's score, is predicated on a significant number of people falling through administrative gaps.

What happens to those people? They don't disappear from the healthcare system. They lose coverage and continue to get sick. They defer preventive care and show up in emergency departments — the most expensive possible setting for treating conditions that could have been managed cheaply earlier. Emergency department visits by uninsured patients generate uncompensated care costs that are ultimately absorbed by hospitals, passed on to insured patients through cost-shifting, or borne by state and local governments through indigent care programs.

The CBO projects the law will increase the number of uninsured Americans by 17 million by 2034, including 5.2 million attributable to work requirements. Some portion of those 17 million will qualify for marketplace coverage and purchase it — though the law also makes changes to premium tax credits that will reduce marketplace affordability. Many will simply be uninsured. The downstream costs of that — in emergency care, in preventable hospitalizations, in untreated chronic conditions — are real but don't appear in the $911 billion savings figure.[1]

The Medical Frailty Gap

The June 1 interim final rule on medical frailty is where much of the implementation action will be concentrated in the months ahead, and its gaps are already being identified by health policy analysts.

The law's definition of medical frailty incorporates several conditions — functional limitations, serious mental illness, chronic conditions affecting multiple organ systems — but the interim final rule gives states significant flexibility in how they operationalize these categories. KFF analysts examining the rule have flagged several points of concern: states that rely only on claims data will miss individuals with relevant conditions who haven't recently accessed care; the ICD-10 and CPT code lists states are using vary significantly and may not capture the full scope of conditions the statute intended to exempt; and the periodic re-verification requirement means someone who is correctly identified as medically frail at enrollment must continue to document that status at each renewal, creating ongoing administrative burden for people who, by definition, have health problems.[4][8]

There's also a timing problem. The interim final rule was issued June 1. Montana goes live July 1. The interval between CMS guidance and state go-live is, for some states, less than a month — not enough time to fully update eligibility systems, train caseworkers, or educate enrollees about their rights.

What the Next Six Months Will Show

By the end of 2026, there will be enough state-level data to begin tracking actual disenrollment numbers against CBO projections. Nebraska's early implementation will offer the first real-world read on how many people are losing coverage, for what reasons, and in what demographic groups.

Public health researchers have been planning surveillance studies, and advocacy organizations have already set up hotlines and outreach programs in early-implementation states. The picture that emerges from Nebraska, Montana, and Iowa between now and January 2027 will shape how — and whether — the remaining states tailor their implementation in ways that minimize or maximize coverage disruption.

The honest answer is that no one knows exactly how many people will lose Medicaid coverage under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act's work requirements, in part because the answer depends on implementation decisions that states are still making. The range of estimates — 5.2 million at the low end, attributable specifically to work requirements, versus 17 million total losing Medicaid by 2034 from all provisions combined — represents a span of outcomes that are all possible, depending on how those decisions go. The states that moved earliest will begin answering the question this summer. The rest of the country will be watching.
