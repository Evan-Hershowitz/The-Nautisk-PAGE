Supreme Court Rules Cancer Patients Cannot Sue Monsanto Over Roundup Under State Law

A 7-2 decision holds that federal pesticide rules preempt state failure-to-warn lawsuits, closing the main legal avenue for thousands of people who say Roundup caused their cancer.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

The Supreme Court ruled Thursday that people who develop cancer after using Roundup weed killer cannot bring state-law lawsuits claiming Monsanto failed to warn them of the risks. The 7-2 decision in Monsanto v. Durnell holds that federal pesticide labeling law overrides those state claims — potentially invalidating thousands of pending cases and saving Bayer, Monsanto's parent company, billions of dollars in avoided liability.

Background

Roundup is one of the world's most widely used herbicides. Its active ingredient, glyphosate, has been at the center of a long-running scientific and legal dispute. The International Agency for Research on Cancer, a World Health Organization body, classified glyphosate as a probable human carcinogen in 2015. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, however, has consistently maintained that Roundup's label does not need a cancer warning.

Tens of thousands of people have sued Monsanto — now owned by German pharmaceutical giant Bayer — arguing that the company knew about possible cancer risks and failed to disclose them. Bayer has paid billions of dollars to settle many of those cases. John Durnell, a Missouri man who used Roundup for about 20 years before developing non-Hodgkin's lymphoma — a cancer that originates in the immune system's lymph nodes — won a $1.25 million verdict in state court. Monsanto appealed, and the case reached the Supreme Court.

What happened

The majority opinion, written by Justice Brett Kavanaugh, sided with Monsanto. The Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act — known as FIFRA — requires that pesticide labels comply with EPA-approved standards. The court held that a state court cannot impose an additional or different warning requirement through a lawsuit. Because Durnell's claim would effectively require Monsanto to add a cancer warning the EPA has not approved, federal law blocks it.

The technical term for this is preemption: when a federal law occupies a regulatory field, it can override state laws that conflict with it. Here, the court held that FIFRA's labeling requirements preempt any state-law demand for a different or stronger warning.

Justices Ketanji Brown Jackson and Neil Gorsuch dissented, arguing the majority misread FIFRA and left a seriously harmed individual without any legal remedy.

Why it matters

The ruling effectively closes the courtroom door for most Roundup cancer plaintiffs who have not yet settled. It could invalidate thousands of pending state-law claims nationwide, dramatically reducing Bayer's remaining legal exposure. Bayer stock is expected to benefit.

For everyday people, the decision raises a troubling question: if a federal agency decides a product does not need a warning, can consumers still hold the manufacturer accountable if they are harmed? After Thursday's ruling, the answer is largely no — at least for federally regulated pesticides. Consumer advocates warn that this strips away an important accountability tool that has historically prompted companies to disclose risks they might prefer to downplay.

The ruling's reach extends beyond Roundup. Its logic could apply to other products governed by federal labeling requirements, potentially shielding manufacturers of certain drugs, food additives, and other regulated products from state-law failure-to-warn claims.

What comes next

Plaintiff attorneys will assess which remaining claims survive under federal legal theories rather than state law — a narrower but not entirely foreclosed path. Congress could amend FIFRA to preserve state-law claims, but there is little sign of that happening. Bayer has said it will continue to defend Roundup's safety record. For the thousands of plaintiffs whose cases were pending, most will likely now settle at reduced amounts or see their claims dismissed.

The bottom line

The Supreme Court shut the door on state-law cancer lawsuits against Roundup, handing Bayer a sweeping legal victory that could be worth billions of dollars in avoided liability.
