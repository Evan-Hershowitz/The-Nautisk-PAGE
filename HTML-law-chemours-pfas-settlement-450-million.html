Chemours Agrees to Pay $450 Million Over PFAS Contamination in Landmark Federal Settlement

The deal — covering facilities in three states — marks the first time the federal government has held a PFAS manufacturer comprehensively accountable for contamination from its production sites.

TN
The Nautisk
June 26, 2026  ·  4 min read

Chemical manufacturer Chemours agreed on Wednesday to pay more than $450 million to settle federal and state claims over its release of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — a class of synthetic chemicals known as PFAS — at facilities across New Jersey, North Carolina, and West Virginia. The Department of Justice and the Environmental Protection Agency announced the deal as the first comprehensive federal enforcement action to hold a PFAS manufacturer directly accountable for contamination from its own production operations.

Background

PFAS compounds have been manufactured since the 1940s and appear in a wide range of consumer and industrial products: non-stick cookware coatings, firefighting foam, food packaging, and stain-resistant fabrics. They are called "forever chemicals" because they do not break down naturally in the environment or in the human body. Epidemiological studies — research that tracks health outcomes across large populations — have linked long-term PFAS exposure to increased risks of certain cancers, thyroid disease, immune dysfunction, and developmental problems in children.

Chemours was spun off from DuPont in 2015 and inherited a significant share of that company's PFAS-related liability. It has faced lawsuits from states, municipalities, and private landowners over contamination from its operations, and has previously reached state-level settlements.

What Happened

The settlement covers four facilities: Chambers Works and Parlin in New Jersey, Fayetteville Works in North Carolina, and Washington Works in West Virginia — all long-standing subjects of environmental litigation and community health concern. The deal has three parts. Chemours will pay a civil penalty of $22.5 million for violating environmental laws. It will spend $90 million over fifteen years on measures to reduce ongoing PFAS discharges across all three states. The largest portion — an estimated $280 million — will fund clean drinking water systems for communities near the contaminated facilities in West Virginia and New Jersey, a direct acknowledgment that the company's operations have reached residential water supplies.

The settlement does not require Chemours to stop manufacturing PFAS entirely. The company may continue producing the chemicals for applications including semiconductor fabrication and certain defense technologies, subject to enhanced controls to prevent future releases. Environmental advocates praised the settlement's scope but called it a starting point rather than a conclusion. The broader PFAS contamination landscape involves scores of manufacturers, military bases, and industrial sites — most left untouched by this agreement.

What This Means

For residents near the four named facilities, the most immediate consequence is concrete: a funded supply of clean drinking water, which some communities have sought for years. More broadly, the settlement signals that federal regulators are prepared to pursue large, multi-state enforcement actions against PFAS manufacturers — not just leaving that work to state attorneys general acting alone.

That matters because PFAS contamination is a national problem. The chemicals have been found in drinking water supplies across the country, and the scale of remediation required dwarfs any single settlement. The Chemours deal establishes a legal and financial precedent, but advocates are explicit: far more enforcement will be needed before the full scope of "forever chemical" harm is addressed.

The Bottom Line The $450 million settlement is the largest federal PFAS enforcement action to date, but with contamination spanning hundreds of sites nationwide, advocates and regulators alike say it is the beginning of a long reckoning — not the end of one.
