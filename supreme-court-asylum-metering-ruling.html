Supreme Court Rules 6-3 That Government May Block Asylum Seekers by Preventing Border Entry

The decision allows federal officials to keep people waiting indefinitely in Mexico, preventing them from filing asylum claims without triggering the legal protections that apply inside the U.S.

TN
The Nautisk
June 26, 2026  ·  4 min read

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6-3 on Wednesday that the federal government may legally prevent people from applying for asylum by keeping them from physically entering U.S. territory. The ruling, in Mullin v. Al Otro Lado (No. 25-5), removes a judicial barrier to a border enforcement practice that lower courts had blocked.

Background

Asylum is a form of legal protection the United States offers to people who have fled persecution based on race, religion, nationality, political opinion, or membership in a social group. Federal law — the Immigration and Nationality Act, or INA — provides that people who arrive in the United States may apply for asylum. The case posed a direct question: does that protection apply to someone who tries to present at an official border crossing but is physically prevented from doing so?

The practice at the center of the case is called metering. Under metering, immigration officials stand at ports of entry along the U.S.-Mexico border and limit how many people may cross to request asylum each day. Those turned away must wait in Mexico — sometimes weeks or months — until space is available. Lower courts had ruled that physically preventing people from crossing could not be used to deny them access to the legal asylum process.

What Happened

The Supreme Court majority disagreed. Writing for the six justices in the majority, the Court held that the INA's text means what it says: you must be physically inside the United States to invoke its protections. If officials prevent someone from crossing, the law does not apply to that person. The three justices in the minority dissented, arguing the ruling empties the asylum system of its practical meaning for people who follow the legal process — presenting at an official border crossing rather than crossing illegally — and are still denied the ability to file a claim.

On the same day, the Court issued a separate ruling allowing the administration to end Temporary Protected Status — a humanitarian designation that shields people from deportation when their home countries are deemed unsafe — for approximately 350,000 Haitians and 6,000 Syrians. Immigration advocates announced plans to pursue legal challenges on related grounds, though no near-term reversal appears likely given the Court's current composition.

What This Means

For people fleeing danger and seeking U.S. protection through the official pathway — presenting at a border crossing — this ruling means that process no longer guarantees the ability to file an asylum claim. Officials can decline to let someone cross. Without entering U.S. territory, that person has no legal standing under the INA to file. The practical effect is the ability to limit asylum applications dramatically — not by changing the law, but by controlling physical access to the country.

Combined with Wednesday's Temporary Protected Status ruling, the Court has authorized a significant contraction of the humanitarian immigration protections that have been part of U.S. law for decades. The two decisions together affect hundreds of thousands of people currently in the country and could affect millions more who might seek entry in the years ahead.

The Bottom Line Metering has a history across administrations — used occasionally under President Obama, expanded under the first Trump administration, rescinded by President Biden in 2021 — and Wednesday's ruling clears the legal path for its broadest application yet.
