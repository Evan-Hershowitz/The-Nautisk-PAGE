Italy's Largest Bank Makes $35 Billion Bid for the World's Oldest Bank, Creating a European Giant

If completed, the deal between Intesa Sanpaolo and Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena would be the biggest merger in Italian banking history.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

Intesa Sanpaolo, Italy's largest bank, launched a €30.6 billion bid — approximately $35 billion — to acquire Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena, the world's oldest surviving bank. The deal, launched in early June and pending regulatory approval, would reshape European banking and create a combined institution second in size only to Spain's Santander among eurozone lenders.

Background

Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena, known as MPS, was founded in 1472 in the Tuscan city of Siena — making it the world's oldest operating bank. Its 550-year history has not protected it from modern financial crises. MPS nearly collapsed during the European debt crisis of the early 2010s — a period when several eurozone countries faced the prospect of defaulting on government debt, which destabilized banks that held that debt — and required repeated government bailouts. The Italian state spent billions of euros to keep it afloat. The government spent years trying to find a buyer willing to absorb MPS's troubled loan portfolio and restructure its operations.

Intesa Sanpaolo, by contrast, has emerged as one of Europe's strongest large banks over the same period — well-capitalized, with a track record of successful acquisitions and a strong retail banking franchise. Italian banking as a whole has been going through a prolonged consolidation phase, with smaller banks struggling against rising digital competition and narrowing interest margins.

What happened

Intesa launched the formal bid on June 8, offering MPS shareholders 16 newly issued Intesa shares for every 10 MPS shares they hold, plus €1 in cash per share. The offer carries a 12.5 percent premium to MPS's closing price before the announcement — meaning Intesa is paying 12.5 percent more than the market price as an incentive for shareholders to accept. The move came one day after a competing offer: Italian lender Banco BPM had approached MPS about a merger of equals that would have created a group with a combined market value of around €50 billion.

To address expected regulatory concerns about market concentration — meaning the deal making Intesa too dominant in the Italian banking market — the bank has already arranged to sell 635 MPS branches and the MPS brand to BPER Banca, a regional Italian lender, if the deal closes. The combined Intesa-MPS group would be valued at approximately €126 billion and is projected to generate €2.9 billion in annual pre-tax cost savings by 2029. The transaction is structured to close by December 2026.

Why it matters

For the Italian economy, the deal signals growing confidence in the banking sector after years of fragility. A stronger, better-capitalized Italian banking system makes it easier for Italian businesses to borrow and invest, and reduces the risk of future taxpayer-funded bailouts like those that drained public funds during MPS's troubled years.

For international observers, the deal is part of a broader wave of European banking consolidation. Regulators and policymakers in Brussels have long encouraged European banks to grow larger and stronger to compete with U.S. financial giants like JPMorgan Chase, Goldman Sachs, and Bank of America, which have dominated global investment banking for decades. A deal of this scale — creating a eurozone institution valued at €126 billion — advances that goal meaningfully.

For MPS itself, a successful acquisition ends years of uncertainty. The bank's employees, depositors, and shareholders have operated under a cloud of doubt about its long-term viability for much of the past decade.

What comes next

The bid requires approval from Italian regulators, the European Central Bank — the institution that supervises large eurozone banks — and competition authorities. If regulatory review proceeds on schedule, the merger could close before the end of 2026. MPS shareholders will vote on the offer in the coming months. If the deal closes, Intesa says it will complete integration and begin realizing cost savings in 2027.

The bottom line

Italy's largest bank is making a $35 billion bet that European banking consolidation is overdue — and the world's oldest bank may finally find a stable home as a result.
