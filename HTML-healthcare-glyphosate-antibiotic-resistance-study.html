Widely Used Weedkiller May Be Accelerating Antibiotic Resistance, Study Finds

Researchers found that hospital bacteria resistant to last-resort antibiotics were also resistant to glyphosate, suggesting a biochemical link between agricultural herbicide use and drug-resistant infections.

TN
The Nautisk
June 26, 2026  ·  4 min read

A study published this month in the journal Frontiers in Microbiology found that bacteria resistant to glyphosate — the world's most widely used weedkiller — are also significantly more resistant to antibiotics, including drugs reserved for the most dangerous infections. Researchers at the University of Buenos Aires collected bacterial strains from both agricultural soils and hospital patients, finding that the same genetic changes protecting bacteria from the herbicide appear to make them harder to treat with drugs.

Background

Glyphosate is the active ingredient in Roundup, the herbicide developed by Monsanto and now produced by Bayer following its acquisition. It is sprayed across hundreds of millions of acres of farmland worldwide each year. Its safety profile has been contested for more than a decade: the World Health Organization's cancer research arm classified it as a "probable human carcinogen" in 2015, a finding that generated extensive litigation. Bayer has paid billions in settlements while maintaining the product is safe when used as directed.

Antibiotic resistance — the ability of bacteria to survive drugs designed to kill them — is already recognized as one of the most serious threats in global public health. The World Health Organization has warned that drug-resistant infections, if unchecked, could kill millions annually within decades. Most regulatory attention has focused on overuse of antibiotics in medicine and agriculture as the primary driver. The glyphosate findings point to a separate and poorly understood accelerant.

What Happened

The Frontiers in Microbiology study tested bacterial strains from Argentine agricultural soils and hospital patients. Every hospital strain proved highly resistant to glyphosate. Those same strains were resistant to between one and sixteen of the antibiotics tested — and 74% were resistant to carbapenems, a class of broad-spectrum antibiotics that doctors deploy only when all other treatments have failed. The mechanism is biochemical, not coincidental: developing resistance to glyphosate triggers genetic changes that also confer resistance to certain antibiotics.

Researchers also found evidence that resistant strains travel in both directions between farms and hospitals, carried by wastewater, runoff, and other environmental pathways. A resistant strain originating on a farm can reach a hospital ward. A resistant strain evolving in a hospital can return to agricultural soil. The study's authors called for greater integration between agricultural policy and antimicrobial resistance — AMR — strategy.

What This Means

Most people are aware that antibiotics in livestock farming can drive resistance — that pathway is already recognized and increasingly regulated. The glyphosate connection suggests a separate and much larger-scale pathway: a weedkiller applied to hundreds of millions of acres may be training bacteria to resist the drugs we rely on to treat serious infections.

If these findings hold up in wider research, regulators in both agriculture and healthcare will face pressure to coordinate in ways they currently do not. For patients, the more immediate concern is practical: the pipeline of antibiotics effective against drug-resistant bacteria is already thin, and findings like this one underscore how many different forces may be depleting it.

The Bottom Line The study does not prove causation at a population level, but it provides a biochemical mechanism for a connection many researchers have suspected — and adds urgency to calls for agricultural and public health policy to be developed together rather than in separate silos.
