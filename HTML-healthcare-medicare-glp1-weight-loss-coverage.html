Medicare Begins Covering GLP-1 Weight-Loss Drugs Today, Capping Copays at $50

A new CMS demonstration program opens access to Wegovy and Zepbound for millions of seniors who meet specific health thresholds, running through the end of 2027.

July 1, 2026 · 4 min read
Share

Millions of Medicare beneficiaries can now obtain GLP-1 weight-loss medications for a flat $50 monthly copay, under a new federal demonstration program that took effect Wednesday. GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) drugs are a class of medications, including semaglutide and tirzepatide, that were originally developed for diabetes and have since proven highly effective for weight loss. Federal law has historically barred Medicare from covering these drugs for weight loss specifically, leaving beneficiaries facing list prices that can exceed $1,000 a month.

Background

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) is running the program, called the Medicare GLP-1 Bridge, as a time-limited pilot rather than a permanent benefit change, because current law still does not allow Medicare Part D — the Medicare program that covers prescription drugs — to cover medications prescribed purely for weight loss. The Bridge works around that restriction by tying eligibility to specific medical conditions linked to obesity, rather than weight loss alone. It is scheduled to run from July 1, 2026, through December 31, 2027, giving CMS roughly 18 months to collect data on cost, utilization, and health outcomes before Congress decides whether to make broader GLP-1 coverage permanent.

Drugmakers have spent years lobbying for exactly this kind of opening. Novo Nordisk, which makes Wegovy, and Eli Lilly, which makes Zepbound, have both pointed to mounting clinical evidence that GLP-1 drugs reduce rates of heart attack, stroke, and other complications — benefits that extend well beyond the number on a scale.

What Happened

To qualify, a beneficiary must be enrolled in Medicare Part D and meet one of three clinical pathways. The most permissive requires a body mass index (BMI) of 35 or higher, regardless of other conditions. A second pathway covers beneficiaries with a BMI of 30 or higher who also have heart failure, uncontrolled high blood pressure, or chronic kidney disease. A third and broadest pathway covers those with a BMI as low as 27 if they have pre-diabetes, a prior heart attack or stroke, or symptomatic peripheral artery disease. CMS has said it will evaluate a patient's BMI at the time they first started GLP-1 therapy, not at the time of the current application, meaning beneficiaries already taking the drugs off-label can qualify retroactively.

The drugs covered include all formulations of Wegovy and Foundayo (orforglipron, Eli Lilly's newer once-daily weight-loss pill), plus the KwikPen version of Zepbound, a prefilled injector device. Notably, Ozempic — widely prescribed for Type 2 diabetes but also sought off-label for weight loss — is excluded from the Bridge, because diabetes patients are already eligible for GLP-1 coverage through standard Part D benefits. The program runs on a separate track from ordinary Part D coverage: the $50 copay does not count toward the standard Part D deductible or the $2,100 annual out-of-pocket spending cap for 2026, and low-income subsidies do not apply to it.

Reactions

Health advocates called the launch historic. The National Council on Aging estimated that hundreds of thousands of seniors previously priced out of GLP-1 therapy could qualify on day one. Novo Nordisk confirmed that expanded access under the Bridge took effect as scheduled Wednesday. Some patient groups have pushed back on the program's exclusions, noting that beneficiaries who don't yet have a qualifying complication — but are still clinically obese — may face higher hurdles than those who do.

Drugs that could cost more than $12,000 a year out of pocket are now available for $600 a year.

What This Means

For eligible seniors, the practical effect is immediate: drugs that could cost more than $12,000 a year out of pocket are now available for $600 a year, provided a doctor documents the right diagnosis. The program's temporary, data-gathering design also matters. It signals that federal officials remain undecided about whether GLP-1 coverage for weight loss should become a permanent, non-restricted Medicare benefit — a change that would carry a far larger price tag across the entire Medicare population. The Bridge arrives as other parts of the health safety net are contracting: new Medicaid work requirements under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed in July 2025, are already removing coverage from hundreds of thousands of lower-income Americans elsewhere in the system.

The bottom line: the Bridge is a data-gathering pilot, not a permanent benefit — Congress will decide whether to make GLP-1 weight-loss coverage a lasting part of Medicare once the 18-month trial ends.
