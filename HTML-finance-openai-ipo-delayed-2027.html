OpenAI Delays IPO to 2027 as Market Conditions Fall Short of $1 Trillion Target

After confidentially filing to go public earlier this month, OpenAI reversed course — pointing to a rough post-debut showing by SpaceX and the company's ongoing cash burn as reasons to wait.

TN
The Nautisk
June 26, 2026  ·  4 min read

OpenAI has decided to delay its initial public offering — the first sale of company shares to the public — until 2027. The company had filed confidentially to go public on June 8, making the reversal in less than three weeks both swift and striking. Chief Executive Sam Altman had targeted a valuation of $1 trillion, a figure that would rank OpenAI among the most valuable publicly traded companies ever.

Background

OpenAI, the company behind ChatGPT, has been one of the most closely watched potential IPO candidates in Silicon Valley. Founded in 2015 and restructured over time into a commercial company, it has attracted investment from Microsoft and other major backers. Its most recent private funding round valued it at $157 billion — making the $1 trillion IPO target an ambitious leap.

SpaceX, Elon Musk's rocket and satellite company, debuted on public markets earlier this year with significant fanfare. Its share price rallied sharply at first, then pulled back faster than analysts expected — a cautionary sign for any company eyeing a high-profile market debut.

What Happened

OpenAI's bankers concluded that the SpaceX retreat exposed real limits in what public investors will pay for complex, money-losing technology companies, regardless of how much excitement surrounds them. OpenAI continues to spend heavily on computing infrastructure to develop its next generation of AI models. Its quarterly financial results — which public companies must disclose regularly — would expose that cash burn to investor scrutiny at a moment the company's finances are not at their strongest.

Sarah Friar, OpenAI's chief financial officer, was the most vocal internal voice for patience. Her concern: going public now would saddle the company with quarterly reporting obligations and shareholder pressure at exactly the wrong moment. Markets registered the news with a modest pullback. The Nasdaq Composite — an index that tracks U.S. technology stocks — slipped 0.5%. The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which tracks 30 large U.S. companies, eked out a 0.1% gain. The S&P 500, a broader index of 500 major U.S. companies, ended nearly flat.

What This Means

An IPO would have let everyday investors buy shares in OpenAI for the first time. The delay means that for now, only large institutional and private investors can own a piece of the company. For OpenAI, staying private buys time to improve its finances before facing the scrutiny that comes with being publicly traded.

For the broader technology investment landscape, the episode signals that appetite for ambitious, expensive AI ventures has real limits — even in a moment when artificial intelligence dominates business headlines. Venture capital firms and startup employees who were counting on IPO proceeds to unlock the value of their holdings will have to wait at least another year.

The Bottom Line Prediction market platform Kalshi places a 59% probability on OpenAI announcing an IPO by March 1, 2027, and a 73% probability by June of that year — meaning the company will almost certainly attempt to go public eventually, but not before investors, employees, and the public face at least another year of waiting.
