You've Been Getting Your News From Instagram. Here's What the Data Actually Shows.

Social media has overtaken TV as the primary global news source, Instagram leads for 18-to-24-year-olds, and AI chatbots for news tripled in two years — the Reuters Institute report describes what's happening without pretending it has an easy fix.

June 27, 2026 · 4 min read
Share

Instagram is now the most-used news platform for 18-to-24-year-olds. Not the most popular social media app — the most popular news platform. More than any newspaper website, more than cable news, more than any broadcaster's app.

That's one of the findings from the 2026 Reuters Institute Digital News Report, which surveyed nearly 100,000 people across 48 countries this spring. It's the most comprehensive annual study of news consumption that exists, and the headline this year is hard to spin in either direction: social media has officially overtaken television as the primary source of news globally. For people under 35, it's not close.

What the numbers say

For 18-to-24-year-olds, Instagram is used for news by 42% of respondents in the study. TikTok is up. X (formerly Twitter) dropped to 15% for news use in that age group, down from where it was a few years ago when it was still seen as the place where news broke first.

More than half of 18-to-24-year-olds in the survey said their main source of news is social media, video networks, or AI chatbots. The video piece is especially pronounced: 73% of people in that age group said they watched a short-form news video at least once a week. The kind of news that works on that format tends to be fast, emotionally resonant, and accessible without context — which describes some kinds of news and definitely not others.

The AI chatbot number is the one that will keep moving. Right now, 17% of the youngest group in the Reuters survey says they use AI chatbots for news — three times the rate of the oldest group. That figure was essentially zero a few years ago. It's accelerating.

Why this matters and why it doesn't

The alarm-bell version of this story is: algorithms are serving people content that confirms what they already believe, traditional journalism is dying, and the result is a population that doesn't know what's actually happening. That version isn't invented. There are real reasons to worry about filter bubbles, about misinformation spreading faster than corrections, about what happens when the economic model for reported news collapses.

But the catastrophizing version skips over something. News consumption has always looked like the technology it traveled on. People in the 1950s got their understanding of the world from television because television existed and was compelling. The generation now coming of age gets it from their phones, from the platforms they're already on, which is exactly as predictable as every media shift that came before it.

What's genuinely different isn't the medium. It's the filtering logic. A TV news broadcast — even a bad one, even one with obvious biases — was edited by people with professional and legal obligations to cover certain stories regardless of whether those stories were satisfying or emotionally engaging. An algorithm surfaces content based on what kept you watching last time. That's not the same editorial principle. And the Reuters report doesn't pretend otherwise: it frames the current moment as "unsettled time" for journalism and news consumption alike.

The chatbot question

The AI piece of this is worth thinking about separately. When you encounter a misleading Instagram post, you can at least click through to the source and check it. When you ask an AI chatbot a question about current events and it gives you a confident, well-organized answer, the chain of accountability is much harder to follow. Chatbots can be wrong with the same tone and fluency they use when they're right.

That doesn't mean chatbots are useless for news — sometimes they're genuinely helpful for synthesizing what's happening. It means the skill of knowing when to verify what you're being told is more important now than it was when your news came from a byline you could look up.

None of this is a case for avoiding Instagram or deleting TikTok. It's more like knowing what the roads are like before you drive on them. The Reuters data isn't an indictment of how young people get information. It's a description. The useful question isn't whether social media is a good or bad place to get news — it's what you do with that information once you have it.
