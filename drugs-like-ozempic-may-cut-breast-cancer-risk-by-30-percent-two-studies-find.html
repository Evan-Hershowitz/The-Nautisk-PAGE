Drugs Like Ozempic May Cut Breast Cancer Risk by 30 Percent, Two Studies Find

The findings, presented at a major cancer conference, add to growing evidence that GLP-1 medications have significant benefits beyond weight loss.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

Two new studies find that GLP-1 medications — the class of drugs that includes Ozempic and Wegovy — are associated with roughly a 30 percent reduction in breast cancer risk. The findings were presented at the 2026 annual meeting of the American Society of Clinical Oncology, the country's most prominent gathering of cancer researchers, and have generated significant discussion in the medical community.

Background

GLP-1 drugs, or glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists, were originally developed to treat type 2 diabetes. They work by mimicking a hormone that regulates blood sugar and appetite, making people feel full sooner and reducing overall calorie intake. Ozempic and Wegovy both contain semaglutide; Mounjaro and Zepbound contain a related compound called tirzepatide. Tens of millions of Americans now use them, and the drugs have reshaped conversations about obesity, metabolism, and chronic disease.

Obesity is a known risk factor for breast cancer — excess body fat raises levels of estrogen and other hormones that can fuel tumor growth. As GLP-1 use has expanded dramatically, researchers have been asking a natural question: do these drugs reduce cancer risk, and if so, is it entirely because of weight loss — or is something else happening at the biological level?

What happened

The two studies together analyzed health records from more than 111,000 women. Both found that women taking GLP-1 medications had roughly 30 percent lower odds of developing breast cancer compared with non-users. One analysis from a separate dataset found an even larger reduction — approximately 47 percent — in a specific patient group.

A key finding: the association held after researchers adjusted for weight and body mass index. That suggests the drugs' effect is not entirely explained by helping women lose weight. Researchers are now investigating whether GLP-1 drugs reduce inflammation, alter hormone pathways, or affect cancer cell behavior directly.

The studies also found the effect consistent across different ages, races, and whether women had diabetes — reducing the likelihood that the results stem from differences in patient populations rather than the drugs themselves.

Why it matters

Breast cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer among American women, with roughly 300,000 new cases each year. Any intervention that measurably reduces risk would be enormously significant for public health.

These studies do not prove that GLP-1 drugs prevent breast cancer. They are observational — meaning they identify a correlation but cannot confirm that the drugs cause the risk reduction. Only randomized controlled trials — studies where some patients are randomly assigned to receive the drug and others are not — can establish causation with certainty. Without that step, researchers cannot rule out that some other shared characteristic of GLP-1 users is driving the result.

Still, the size and consistency of the association across multiple large datasets is attracting serious scientific attention. For the many millions of women already taking these drugs for diabetes or weight management, the findings add to a rapidly growing list of potential secondary benefits, which already includes documented reductions in heart disease, kidney disease, and alcohol dependence.

What comes next

Researchers are calling for dedicated clinical trials to test whether GLP-1 drugs can actively prevent breast cancer. Those trials would take years to design, run, and analyze. In the meantime, oncologists are watching these findings closely as more data emerges from real-world use. The FDA has not approved any GLP-1 drug for cancer prevention, and doctors are not currently prescribing them for that purpose.

The bottom line

Two major studies suggest Ozempic-class drugs may reduce breast cancer risk by about 30 percent — a striking finding that researchers say demands further investigation.
