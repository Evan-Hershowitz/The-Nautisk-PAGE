Apple Hikes MacBook and iPad Prices, Sending Its Stock Down 6% and Dragging Nasdaq Lower

A global memory chip shortage driven by AI demand is pushing up consumer electronics costs, with Apple taking the most visible hit among major tech companies.

TN
The Nautisk Independent journalism — thenautisk.com

Apple announced price increases of up to $200 on its MacBook and iPad product lines Thursday, blaming a severe shortage of memory chips fueled by artificial intelligence demand. Its stock fell more than 6 percent, wiping roughly $265 billion in market value — the total worth of a company's outstanding shares — in a single day, and extended the Nasdaq's losing streak to four consecutive sessions.

Background

The global market for memory chips — the components that allow computers and tablets to run multiple applications simultaneously and store data temporarily — has been thrown into disruption by surging demand from artificial intelligence. Training and running AI systems requires enormous amounts of memory, and chip manufacturers have been unable to produce it fast enough to keep pace.

Industry analysts have taken to calling the shortage "RAMageddon." According to TrendForce, a semiconductor research firm, contract prices for conventional memory chips rose between 90 and 95 percent in the first quarter of 2026, with further increases of 58 to 63 percent expected in the current quarter. Memory is a major cost component in laptops and tablets, and that price surge is now flowing through to consumers.

What happened

Apple raised prices on five product lines: the MacBook Neo, MacBook Air, MacBook Pro, iPad Air, and iPad Pro. Some models increased by as much as $200. Prices on iPhones, Apple Watches, and AirPods were not changed. Apple cited AI-fueled chip demand directly, saying memory costs had reached a level it could no longer absorb.

Investors responded harshly. Apple's shares fell 6.12 percent — its worst single-day decline in more than a year — erasing roughly $265 billion in market capitalization. The company's total market value remains above $4 trillion.

The decline dragged the Nasdaq Composite — the index that tracks the performance of major technology stocks — down 0.46 percent to 25,358, marking its fourth consecutive losing session and its longest such stretch since February. The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which tracks a broader mix of 30 large American companies, edged up 71 points to close at 51,920, buoyed by strong earnings from other sectors.

Why it matters

Apple's price hikes are a concrete sign to consumers that the AI infrastructure buildout has real costs — and that those costs are arriving in stores. For a family buying a new laptop or tablet this summer, the bill is meaningfully higher than it was a year ago.

For investors, the market reaction reflects deeper anxiety about Apple's trajectory. When Apple raises prices, some buyers delay purchases — a dynamic that can weigh on revenue in future quarters. Apple generated more than $400 billion in revenue last year. Even a modest slowdown in device sales creates material risk for a company of its size.

The same day Apple announced higher prices, Micron Technology — one of the world's largest memory chip makers — reported record quarterly earnings driven by the exact AI demand squeezing Apple. One company's bonanza is another's cost problem — and ultimately, the consumer pays.

What comes next

Other device makers facing the same memory cost pressures — including PC manufacturers and Android tablet makers — may announce similar price increases in coming weeks. Chip companies are accelerating plans to expand production capacity. Analysts expect memory prices to begin easing in 2027 as new supply comes online, but a rapid reversal of today's price increases is unlikely before then. For now, consumers shopping for new Apple hardware should expect to pay more.

The bottom line

Apple's price hikes on MacBooks and iPads confirm that the AI chip shortage has reached the everyday consumer — and markets penalized Apple sharply for being the first major tech company to say so publicly.
