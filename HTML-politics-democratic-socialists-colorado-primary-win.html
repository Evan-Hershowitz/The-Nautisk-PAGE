Democratic Socialists Extend Primary Winning Streak, Toppling a 30-Year Colorado Incumbent

Melat Kiros's defeat of Rep. Diana DeGette follows a wave of insurgent wins in New York, intensifying a fight over the Democratic Party's direction ahead of November.

July 1, 2026 · 4 min read
Share

Colorado Democrats delivered the party establishment another jolt Tuesday, as 29-year-old Melat Kiros defeated 30-year incumbent Rep. Diana DeGette in the primary for Colorado's 1st Congressional District — the latest and most striking example of a leftward insurgency reshaping Democratic primaries across the country this year. Kiros ran as a democratic socialist, a label for candidates who campaign to the left of the traditional Democratic Party establishment on policies such as Medicare for All, stronger labor protections, and higher taxes on the wealthy.

Background

The Colorado results followed a similar upheaval in New York the week prior, where two self-described democratic socialists and a progressive candidate — several running with the backing of New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani's political network — defeated establishment-favored candidates, including sitting incumbents, in Democratic House primaries. That earlier wave energized a movement that had struggled for years to translate primary-night enthusiasm into actual wins against entrenched incumbents.

Colorado's primary contests were widely viewed as a test of whether the New York results reflected a durable national trend or a one-off product of local politics. DeGette, first elected in 1996, has long been considered a reliably progressive vote in her own right, which made her vulnerability to a challenger further to her left especially notable to party strategists trying to read the electorate's mood.

What Happened

Kiros, a first-time candidate and former attorney endorsed by Sen. Bernie Sanders, ran on an explicitly democratic socialist platform and argued that DeGette's three decades in Congress amounted to ineffectiveness rather than experience. DeGette countered that institutional knowledge is precisely what's needed to counter Trump. Kiros had already signaled her strength in March, when she nearly outpaced DeGette in the state's pre-primary assembly process despite never having run for office before.

Elsewhere on Colorado's ballot, the establishment fared better. Sen. John Hickenlooper, a comparatively centrist former governor, turned back a challenge from state Sen. Julie Gonzales, who ran as an "insurgent progressive" and disclosed a lapsed past membership in the Democratic Socialists of America, the country's largest socialist organization. In the governor's race, Colorado Attorney General Phil Weiser defeated sitting U.S. Senator Michael Bennet for the Democratic nomination, in a contest defined less by ideology than by dueling claims over who had fought Trump harder during his first term.

Reactions

Party strategists reacted to the Kiros result with a mix of alarm and vindication, depending on their factional lean. Establishment-aligned Democrats warned that a leftward shift in candidate selection could cost the party winnable seats in swing districts come November, pointing to Colorado's 8th Congressional District — currently held by Republican Rep. Gabe Evans — as a seat where ideological positioning could prove decisive. Progressive organizers, by contrast, framed the back-to-back wins in New York and Colorado as evidence that the party's rank-and-file voters are demanding a sharper break from incrementalism, particularly on economic issues.

What This Means

For voters, the practical stakes go beyond any single House seat. A Democratic caucus that increasingly includes democratic socialists alongside centrists heading into the 2026 midterms will have to reconcile genuinely different views on issues from health care to foreign policy to economic regulation — a coalition-management challenge that could shape both the party's messaging and its ability to hold competitive districts. For Republicans, a leftward-moving Democratic primary electorate in safe seats may be read as an opportunity to paint the broader party as out of step with swing voters, even though the affected seats themselves are unlikely to flip. The next test comes in November, when the energy that has fueled primary upsets in deep-blue districts will be measured against the far more competitive map of the general election, where Democrats will need votes well beyond their own base to retake the House.

The bottom line: the real test of this insurgency comes in November, when primary-night energy in safe blue districts meets the far more competitive map of the general election.
