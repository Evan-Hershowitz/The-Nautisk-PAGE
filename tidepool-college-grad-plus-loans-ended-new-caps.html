The Student Loan Rules Just Changed. Here's What That Means If You're Heading to Grad School.

Starting July 1, Grad PLUS loans are gone, graduate borrowing is capped, and the students most affected are the ones who already planned their finances around rules that no longer exist.

June 27, 2026 · 4 min read
Share

On July 1, 2026, the federal government stopped offering a type of loan that hundreds of thousands of graduate and professional students have relied on for decades. The Grad PLUS program is gone. New borrowing caps are now federal law. And most people who will be affected by these changes are still figuring out what it means for them.

The changes come from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, a sweeping piece of legislation signed earlier this year that reshaped federal student lending from the top down. For undergrads, the direct impact is relatively contained. The bigger shift hits anyone planning on graduate school, law school, medical school, or any professional program after their bachelor's degree.

What actually changed

Before July 1, graduate students could borrow Grad PLUS loans — federal loans that covered the full cost of attendance, whatever that number happened to be. There was no annual cap. A student at a private law school might borrow $60,000 a year in Grad PLUS loans alone. A medical student could end up with $300,000 or more in federal debt across four years. The program existed for decades on the logic that advanced degrees led to higher earnings, so higher borrowing made sense.

That logic did not survive the current Congress.

Starting July 1, new graduate borrowers face a $20,500 annual cap on unsubsidized federal loans and a $100,000 lifetime cap. Professional students in medicine, law, and dentistry get slightly more room — $50,000 per year and $200,000 lifetime. But all federal borrowing, across every loan type, now falls under a new $257,500 lifetime ceiling.

For reference: four years of medical school at a private institution typically costs between $250,000 and $350,000 in tuition and living expenses combined.

Who this hits hardest

The immediate impact falls on three groups: people starting a new graduate program in fall 2026, people mid-program who were counting on Grad PLUS for remaining years, and people still deciding whether to pursue an advanced degree at all.

Students currently enrolled have some protection. If you took out a Grad PLUS loan before July 1, you can continue borrowing under the old rules for up to three more years, or until you finish your current program at the same school — whichever comes first. Incoming students get no such cushion.

What fills the gap? The Department of Education's answer is essentially: the market. That means private loans, merit scholarships, institutional aid, or reconsidering which program you attend. Private loans typically come with higher interest rates and fewer borrower protections than federal loans. They're not a 1:1 substitute.

Some schools have started adjusting ahead of the change. A growing list of universities — including Purdue, Johns Hopkins, and Santa Clara — have announced tuition reductions or enhanced scholarships in direct response to the new caps. Others haven't moved yet.

The argument on both sides

The change was designed, at least in part, to address a genuine problem. Grad PLUS made it easy to borrow unlimited amounts, which contributed to runaway tuition growth at professional schools. Law and medical school costs grew faster than inflation for years, in part because the lending was effectively uncapped. The case for the new limits is that schools will now have to compete on price in a way they never had to before.

The case against is that the adjustment happened faster than the system could follow — and the people most exposed are the ones who already made their decisions assuming the old rules. Someone who enrolled in a three-year law program in fall 2025 may have done their financial math with Grad PLUS in the picture. That math is now different.

There's a narrower version of this story for high schoolers and college juniors and seniors thinking about what comes after their undergraduate degree. The calculus on graduate school — which programs are worth the cost, which fields can sustain the debt, and which options exist outside the federal loan system — just shifted in ways that haven't fully shaken out yet. The full picture of what's changing is worth understanding now, before you're sitting in front of an enrollment form.
